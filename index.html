Cost of Gambling the Human Factor in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity, Humans, Technology, MGM breach, Social Engineering

Technology, itself, never makes a mistake. The human programmers that create the technology or instruct the technology what to do are the ones responsible for the errors in technology. Human erors can lead to several different types of cybersecurity concerns. One of the most recent examples of this can be seen throught the Las Vegas MGM resorts breach that occurred in September of 2023. The threat actor was able to breach their database by simply tricking the helpdesk attendant into resetting their password. This is known as social engineering, where attackers manipulate targets into performing certain actions or revealing specific information. MGM had to shut down some of its systems to prevent further damage. This resulted in issues with hotel room digital keys to elevators to slot machines. MGM was losing about $8.4 million in revnue alone during the breach. Caesars Entertainment also had to pay millions of dollars to hackers due to s breach via social engineering that occured around the same time. Social engineering accounts for 98% of all cyber-attacks due to the human factor. Huamn impact keeps cybersecurity going. Elevating the awareness and understanfing of the human factor and vulnerabilities in cybersecurity would result in the most effective security strategy.

Women in Cybersecurity

Women, Cybersecurity

In 2022, women made up 25% of cybersecurity jobs globally. Just 1% make up senior leadership positions. Cybersecurity has an ever-growing variety of threats, thus, diverse perspectives is crucial in this field. American Psychological Association has proven that men and women have the same innate capabilities in math and science. Research shows how women are equally capable to succeed in the cybersecurity field. The only factor creating this gap between the two genders are the stereotypes and biases placed by society. In 2020, cybercrime caused global business a trillion dollar loss. Thus reversing these sterotypes and biases would greatly benefit a plethora of companies. A company is as strong as their line of defense. In cybersecurity, it is crucial that a company's line of defense is diverse by having a range of perspectives, experiences, and knowledge contribute. The Cybersecurity & Infrastructure Security Agency is a great example of what women can do in the cybersecurity field. The agency had a female-leading team that drafted the Blueprint for a SecureCyber Furute, planning and analysis that supports the COVID-19 vaccine rollout, supporting election security efforts, developing recommendations for protecting High Value Asset (HVA) systems across the federal government, and supporting internal diversity and inclusion programs. Companies would highly benefit from support women in pursuing careers in cybersecurity, especially financial benefits.
